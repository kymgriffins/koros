Chapter 01 — The Scale

Kenya's budget is one of Africa's largest. Almost no one can read it.

Every year, Kenya's national government allocates more than KSh 4 trillion in public funds. County governments — all 47 of them — control an additional KSh 500 billion in devolved revenue. That is more money than most Kenyans will earn in multiple lifetimes.

These budgets are, by law, public documents. The Controller of Budget publishes quarterly reports. The Auditor General releases annual findings. The National Treasury posts budget statements online. Everything is technically available.

"The budget is a moral document. It tells you who a government thinks matters — and who it has decided can wait."

02
Chapter 02 — The Gap

The information exists. But it was never designed to be understood.

Kenya's budget documents are written by and for bureaucrats. They are dense, technical, and deliberately obscure. A single Appropriation Bill can run to over 300 pages of code-referenced line items. County budget documents are often posted as poorly-scanned PDFs — if they are posted at all.

The result is a profound transparency gap. Citizens have a legal right to this information. But without the tools to understand it, that right is meaningless. Journalists file FOI requests for data that has been public for years. Activists attend county budget forums without the context needed to ask meaningful questions.

"Transparency without comprehension is just compliance. We want to close the gap between what governments publish and what citizens understand."

03
Chapter 03 — The Cost

When citizens can't follow the money, the money follows itself.

The Auditor General's reports year after year reveal the same patterns: unaccounted-for funds, projects funded but never built, procurement irregularities in plain sight. In FY 2022/23 alone, the Auditor flagged billions in unexplained expenditures across counties.

This is not a problem of information scarcity. Kenya has more public finance data than ever before. It is a problem of access, literacy, and engagement. The missing link is a platform that bridges verified government data with the citizens it serves.

"Every unread audit report is a silent permission slip for waste. Budget Ndio Story exists to read them — loudly."

Chapter 04 — Our Answer

Budget Ndio Story: Kenya's budget, decoded for everyone.

Budget Ndio Story is a platform, a publication, and a civic education project built for the next generation of Kenyan citizens. We do three things: investigate (obtain and verify every major budget document), translate (turn raw data into visual, accessible stories), and activate (build tools for public participation at the county level).

Everything we produce is free. We will never paywall public interest information. From data visualisations of the healthcare budget to ward-level expenditure trackers — if it concerns public money, it belongs to the public.

"Free. Always. Because transparency should never require a subscription."

04 — Our Principles

How We
Work

Three non-negotiables drive everything we publish.

  • Verified first. We never publish unverified budget figures. Every number is cross-referenced against at least two official sources before publication.
  • Always free. Budget transparency is a public good. We will never paywall data, reports, or tools derived from public documents.
  • Citizen-first design. Every product we build is tested with the people who need it most — not the people who already understand fiscal policy.
  • Non-partisan always. Budget data is presented as it is, not as any political narrative would want it to appear. The numbers speak.
